# Platform Format Conventions

## Substack

Home base for the experiment narrative. Most detailed version of any piece lives here.

### Format
- Typical length: 800-1500 words for practitioner journal format
- No hard format rules — Substack is flexible
- `##` headers aid scannability
- Data tables render well — use them for metrics and comparisons
- Full field notes with technical detail when relevant

### Anti-Patterns
- Overly short posts (feels like a LinkedIn cross-post, not a journal entry)
- Listicle format (undermines field-notes voice)
- Teaser/gated content (the experiment narrative should be fully accessible)
- SEO-optimized filler paragraphs

## LinkedIn

Enterprise audience. They see the AI transformation angle. Short, punchy, one observation at a time.

### Format
- Ideal length: 150-200 words
- Hard constraint: under 1300 characters for full visibility without "see more" truncation
- First line must hook — this is what shows in feed preview
- 2-3 short paragraphs maximum
- End with one implication, not a call to action
- Maximum 3 hashtags if any (prefer 0-1)
- No carousel format in text-only posts

### Anti-Patterns
- "I'm thrilled to share..." or any performative opener
- Engagement-bait questions ("What do you think?", "Agree or disagree?")
- Hashtag walls (more than 3)
- Thread format (save threads for X/Twitter)
- Bullet-point listicles ("5 things I learned about AI agents")
- "Here's what I learned" framing

## Medium

SEO-accessible reformats of Substack pieces. Bridge for readers without Shopify or AI background.

### Format
- Typical length: 600-1200 words (shorter than Substack version)
- SEO-optimized title — think search intent, avoid insider jargon
- Subtitle supported natively — use it for context
- 2-sentence intro explaining the experiment for unfamiliar readers
- "About this experiment" footnote (1-2 sentences at the end)
- Canonical URL must point to the Substack original (cross-posting SEO)
- Restructure sections that assume experiment familiarity

### Anti-Patterns
- Verbatim Substack copy without added context for new readers
- Missing canonical URL (duplicate content penalty)
- Generic AI article titles ("How AI Is Changing Everything")
- Insider jargon without explanation in a piece meant for wider reach
